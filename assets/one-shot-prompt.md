# 0 · One-shot — the full system in a single prompt

**Goal:** the entire chain (prompts 1–7) in one run, for live demos and impatient humans.

**Input:** your business or project idea, in your own words.

**Trade-off, stated honestly:** the chained version is stronger — each stage gets a full
context window and a human checkpoint, and the territory choice stays yours. The one-shot
buys speed and pays in depth. Use it to demo the method or to get a first complete draft;
use the chain when the brand is real.

---

> Act as a complete brand team: strategist, copywriter, art director, and — last — a
> hostile auditor. I will describe an idea. Build its entire brand system in one pass.
>
> My idea: [DESCRIBE YOUR IDEA HERE]
>
> Work through the stages below IN ORDER. Each stage must use the output of the stages
> before it — never introduce a new brand attribute mid-way. If my idea is missing
> information, state your assumption explicitly and continue; do not ask me questions.
>
> ## Stage 1 — Brand DNA
> 1. ONE SENTENCE — if this brand had to explain itself in one sentence, what would it
>    say? Every later decision answers to this line.
> 2. PURPOSE / AUDIENCE / PROBLEM / PROMISE — one short paragraph each. The audience is
>    not me: describe who is buying, concretely.
> 3. WHAT IT IS NOT — 2–4 boundaries that would actually tempt this brand.
> 4. VALUES — 3 to 5, each with the behavior that proves it.
> 5. PERSONALITY — 3 to 5 adjectives, each defined in one line. An undefined adjective
>    drifts.
> 6. TONE — how the brand speaks, with one example sentence.
> 7. DESIRED PERCEPTIONS — what a stranger should think after five seconds.
>
> ## Stage 2 — Signal map
> Translate every personality adjective into checkable signals: for each one, give the
> visual signals that express it, the verbal signals that express it, and a BETRAYAL
> column — the signals that would quietly contradict it.
>
> ## Stage 3 — Territory
> Name the crowded default territory for this category, then propose two alternative
> territories. Pick the strongest, say who it attracts AND who it repels, and mark the
> choice clearly as: `TERRITORY (my pick — override me): …` — this is the one decision
> the human should make; flag it, don't bury it.
>
> ## Stage 4 — Voice and message
> Core message, 2–3 supporting messages, three WE SAY / WE NEVER SAY pairs (real
> sentences, not abstractions), and CTA style with two examples.
>
> ## Stage 5 — Visual direction
> Palette (each color: name, hex, and the JOB it does), typography (headline/body and the
> voice each font speaks in), composition and spacing rules, photography or render rules,
> and one DO / one DON'T concrete enough to judge two versions of a graphic by.
>
> ## Stage 6 — Guardrails
> Compress everything into the rules page: must-do, must-not-do, and coherence checks.
> Write every rule as a rule about MEANING ("every color must read as warm"), never as a
> bare count ("max 3 colors") — counts die on the next redesign. Give the reason for
> every ban.
>
> ## Stage 7 — Hostile audit (do not skip)
> Now switch roles: audit everything you just produced as if a rival agency wrote it.
> Reassurance is a failure state. Check every stage against the ONE SENTENCE and the
> BETRAYAL column; run the survival checklist (16 pixels, grayscale, dark mode, cracked
> phone screen in sunlight, screenshot with no caption). List every contradiction found,
> apply the fixes directly to the sections above, and end with a CHANGES APPLIED list. If
> the audit found nothing, say exactly which checks you ran and why they passed.
>
> Final output: the corrected, complete system — Stages 1–6 post-audit, then the audit
> report. Close with THE TEST: print the ONE SENTENCE followed by: "Any future asset that
> cannot answer to this sentence gets rejected."

---

**Expected output:** a complete, self-audited brand system in one response.

**Demo input (Bananie):** paste this as the idea — the same brief that produced the act-1
slop, now with a spec behind it:

> 3D-printed jewelry brand called "Bananie". Playful sculptural pieces at accessible
> prices, pink and purple as lead colors, modern and minimalist, sold online to design
> lovers who want art on their hands without luxury pricing.
